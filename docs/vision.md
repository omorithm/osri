# OSRI Vision

## Overview

OSRI exists to make the resilience of open-source software measurable, understandable, and actionable.

Open-source software has become a foundation of modern technology. Applications, organizations, and critical systems rely on thousands of open-source projects every day. However, understanding whether these projects are healthy, sustainable, and capable of supporting long-term adoption remains a difficult challenge.

OSRI aims to provide insight into the factors that influence the resilience of open-source projects.

---

## The Problem

Software projects do not fail only because of technical issues.

They can become fragile because of:

- declining maintainer activity
- limited contributor diversity
- unclear governance structures
- inconsistent release practices
- dependency risks
- lack of community sustainability

Today, many decisions about adopting open-source software rely heavily on surface-level signals such as:

- GitHub stars
- downloads
- popularity
- recent commits

While useful, these signals do not provide a complete picture of project health.

---

## Our Vision

Our vision is a future where the health and resilience of open-source software can be evaluated through transparent, evidence-based signals.

OSRI aims to help developers, maintainers, organizations, and researchers understand:

- how healthy a project is today
- what factors contribute to its resilience
- what risks may affect its future sustainability

---

## Why Resilience Matters

A popular project is not always a sustainable project.

A project with thousands of users may still depend on a small number of maintainers. A widely adopted dependency may become a critical point of failure if its ecosystem weakens.

OSRI focuses on understanding the conditions that allow open-source projects to endure.

---

## How OSRI Approaches the Problem

OSRI evaluates resilience through multiple dimensions:

### Technical Health

Understanding development activity, releases, and project maintenance signals.

### Community Health

Understanding contributor activity, collaboration patterns, and maintainer sustainability.

### Governance Health

Understanding project structure, documentation, contribution processes, and transparency.

### Ecosystem Impact

Understanding how important a project is within the wider software ecosystem.

---

## Core Principles

### Transparency

OSRI assessments should be explainable and based on observable signals.

### Open Source First

The project should remain open, community-driven, and accessible.

### Evidence Over Assumptions

Metrics and recommendations should be grounded in measurable information.

### Continuous Improvement

Resilience is not a fixed state. OSRI should help identify trends over time.

---

## Long-Term Direction

OSRI aims to become a trusted open-source intelligence platform for understanding software project resilience.

The long-term goal is to help the open-source community identify strengths, recognize risks, and make better decisions about the software infrastructure the world depends on.

---

## What OSRI Is Not

OSRI is not:

- a vulnerability scanner
- a replacement for security analysis tools
- a popularity ranking system
- a judgement system for individual maintainers

OSRI exists to provide context, insight, and transparency.
